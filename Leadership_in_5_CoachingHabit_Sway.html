Say less, ask more, and change the way you lead forever. Here are the 5 questions that will transform how you develop your team.

You've Been Here Before

A team member walks into your office with a problem. Your instinct kicks in — you listen for 30 seconds, and then you start solving. You give advice. You fix it. You move on.

It feels efficient. But here's the problem: you just taught them to come back to you every single time. You became the bottleneck, and they didn't grow.

The single biggest shift you can make as a leader: stop giving answers so quickly, and start asking better questions.

Quick Self-Check

How many of these sound like you?

I usually offer a solution before asking what they've already tried
I feel responsible for having all the answers
My team comes to me with the same types of problems repeatedly
I sometimes solve the wrong problem because I jumped in too fast
If you checked even one, you're normal. And these 5 questions will help.
Question 1 of 5

"What's on your mind?"

The Kickstart Question
WHAT

This is how you open every coaching conversation. It's open enough to let them lead, but focused enough to get somewhere meaningful. It cuts through small talk and gets straight to what actually matters to them right now.

"
An almost fail-safe way to start a chat that's quick and yet focused is to ask: What's on your mind?
— Michael Bungay Stanier, The Coaching Habit
SO WHAT

Most leaders open conversations with "How's it going?" or "Any updates?" — which gives you surface-level answers. This question immediately signals: I'm here, I'm listening, and you're driving this conversation.

AT BREEZE

A new hire is struggling in training. Instead of jumping to "Here's what you need to do," open with "What's on your mind?" You might discover they're overwhelmed by the pace — not the content. That changes your entire approach.

NOW WHAT

Try This Today

In your next 1:1 or check-in, replace your usual opener with "What's on your mind?" Then wait. Let the silence do the work.

✓ Self-Check

How would you handle this?

It's Monday morning. A team member walks into your office and says, "I need to talk to you about the new scheduling system — it's a complete mess." What do you do first?
The Answer

B — "What's on your mind?"

Option A sounds logical — gather information, then solve. But it assumes the scheduling system is the real issue. It might be. Or that complaint might be a proxy for frustration, feeling unheard, or a team dynamic that goes deeper than the system. "What's on your mind?" opens the door without assumptions. The complaint is the knock — this question is you saying: come in, I'm here, you lead.

Question 2 of 5

"And what else?"

The AWE Question
WHAT

The single most powerful coaching question in the world. Three words that keep you from jumping to solutions too early. The first answer someone gives you is rarely the most important one — there's almost always something deeper underneath.

"
The first answer someone gives you is almost never the only answer, and it's rarely the best answer.
— Michael Bungay Stanier, The Coaching Habit
SO WHAT

This question buys you time, keeps the other person thinking, and often reveals the real issue. Ask it at least three times in any conversation. It's your discipline against the "advice monster" — that urge to jump in and fix things.

AT BREEZE

A team member tells you they're frustrated with their schedule. You ask "And what else?" They mention they feel overlooked for a project. You ask again. Turns out — they're questioning whether they have a future here. That's the real conversation.

NOW WHAT

Try This Today

The next time someone brings you a problem, ask "And what else?" at least twice before you offer any input. Count to yourself if you need to.

✓ Self-Check

How would you handle this?

You asked "What's on your mind?" and a team member shared a coworker conflict. You asked "And what else?" — they mentioned workload. You asked again — they brought up their career path. They pause: "I think that's it." What do you do next?
The Answer

C — "What's the real challenge here for you?"

You've done the AWE Question exactly right — three rounds, real depth. Now you have three things on the table: conflict, workload, career. Option A overuses the question (they said they're done — trust that). Option B tries to solve everything at once and scatters the energy. Option C takes everything you've heard and asks them to find the thread that matters most to them right now. That's where coaching happens.

Question 3 of 5

"What's the real challenge
here for you?"

The Focus Question
WHAT

This question does two critical things: it zeroes in on the actual problem (not the first problem they mentioned), and the words "for you" make it personal. You're not solving an abstract issue — you're coaching a person.

"
We often solve the wrong problem by working on the first challenge presented rather than finding the real one.
— Michael Bungay Stanier, The Coaching Habit
SO WHAT

Leaders waste enormous energy solving presenting problems instead of root causes. This question prevents you from building the perfect solution to the wrong challenge. The "for you" is the key — it turns the focus from the situation to the person.

AT BREEZE

Someone on your team isn't performing to expectations. Before you launch into a performance plan, ask: "What's the real challenge here for you?" Maybe the real issue isn't skill — it's that they don't understand what "good" looks like, or they're dealing with something outside of work that's pulling their focus.

NOW WHAT

Try This Today

When someone describes a messy situation with multiple issues, resist solving any of them. Instead ask: "What's the real challenge here for you?" and let their answer guide where you focus.

✓ Self-Check

How would you handle this?

A manager tells you they're dealing with a performance issue, a new process rollout, AND a big schedule change coming up. You ask "What's the real challenge here for you?" — they pause and say: "Honestly... I don't think I have the confidence to handle all of it at once." What do you do next?
The Answer

B — "How can I help?"

They just gave you the real thing — not the tasks on their list, but the feeling underneath them. This is the breakthrough moment in the conversation. Now you need to find out what they actually need from you. Coaching? Resources? A pep talk? Just to be heard? "How can I help?" lets them tell you. Option A feels warm but shuts the moment down. Option C risks over-mining — they already opened up. Option D retreats to logistics when they just got personal.

Question 4 of 5

"How can I help?"

The Lazy Question
WHAT

It's called "lazy" because it forces the other person to make a direct request instead of you guessing what they need. It stops you from over-functioning and taking on work that isn't yours. It puts the ownership back where it belongs — with them.

"
Stop offering help that isn't requested. Ask the other person to make a direct request of you, and then decide if you can fulfill it.
— Michael Bungay Stanier, The Coaching Habit
SO WHAT

Most leaders assume they know what help looks like and jump in. But your idea of help and theirs are often completely different. This question prevents you from doing work they should own and makes sure the help you give is actually what they need.

AT BREEZE

A team member is not accepting feedback well and getting emotional. After you've listened, ask: "How can I help?" Maybe they need you to deliver feedback differently. Maybe they need a minute to process. Maybe they just need to know you're still in their corner. Let them tell you.

NOW WHAT

Try This Today

The next time you catch yourself about to volunteer a solution, pause and ask "How can I help?" instead. You'll be surprised — what they need is often simpler than what you were about to offer.

Question 5 of 5

"What was most useful
for you?"

The Learning Question
WHAT

This is how you close every coaching conversation. It forces the other person to extract their own takeaway — which makes it stick. It also gives you real-time feedback on what's actually landing. Double loop learning in one sentence.

"
People don't really learn when you tell them something. They learn when they have a chance to reflect on what happened.
— Michael Bungay Stanier, The Coaching Habit
SO WHAT

Without reflection, a conversation is just noise. This question turns every interaction into a development moment. It creates a "learning loop" where the other person names what they're taking away — and that act of naming is what makes it stick in their brain long-term.

AT BREEZE

You just worked through a tough issue with a team member — performance, a conflict, a process breakdown. Before they walk away, ask: "What was most useful for you?" Their answer tells you what landed, what to repeat, and whether you actually helped or just talked.

NOW WHAT

Try This Today

End your next meaningful conversation — a 1:1, a coaching moment, even a team meeting — by asking "What was most useful for you?" Make it your new closing ritual.
