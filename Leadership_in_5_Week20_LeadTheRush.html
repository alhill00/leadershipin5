3 Things Great Leaders Do This Week
1

Be visible — not just available

There's a difference between being reachable and being present. This week, get out of your office or off your screen and actually show up where your team is working. You don't have to say anything profound. Just being there signals: I see you, I'm in this with you.

"I'm going to walk the floor for 10 minutes before the morning bank."
2

Check in on people, not just performance

During a rush week, leaders naturally track metrics — on-time, turn times, headcount. Don't stop doing that. But also ask a different question: How are you holding up? Four words that cost nothing and mean everything when someone is running on fumes.

"Hey — genuinely, how are you doing this week?"
3

Catch wins in real time

A busy week creates dozens of moments where your team does something right under pressure. Don't let them pass. A quick, specific callout in the moment — "I noticed how you handled that" — is the highest-value thing you can do this week. It costs 20 seconds and lasts for days.

"That recovery on the delayed flight was exactly what great looks like."
✈️

Remember: your team mirrors your energy

When it's chaotic and you're calm, they find a way to be calm. When you're visibly stressed, they amplify it. You don't have to pretend everything is perfect — just be the steady, grounded presence they can orient around.

✓ Self-Check — How Would You Handle This?

What's your move?

It's the middle of a chaotic shift. A gate agent is visibly stressed and clearly falling behind. You walk by and notice. What do you do first?
The Answer
B — "What do you need right now?"

Option A is helpful but assumes you know what kind of help they need. Option C is the wrong moment for a standards reminder — they know. Option D takes ownership away from them and sets a bad precedent. Option B takes five seconds and tells you everything: do they need another set of hands, a word of encouragement, a resource, or just someone to acknowledge that it's hard? Meeting the right need beats solving the wrong problem every time.
